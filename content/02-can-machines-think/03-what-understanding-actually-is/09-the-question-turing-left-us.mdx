Alan Turing died before he could see what the field he helped start would become.

He didn't answer the question he asked. He reframed it, made it testable, pushed it into the open — and then left it there.

Seventy-five years later, we have machines that do things he could not have imagined. They write. They translate. They diagnose. They compose. They hold conversations that fool people consistently.

We still don't know what thinking is.

That's not a scandal. It's what the frontier looks like. The question hasn't gone away — it's gotten sharper. It's sitting in every conversation you have with a language model, every time you wonder whether there's something it understands or something it's only imitating.

Turing would have found that interesting. So should you.
