A computer program is the person in the room.

The program receives inputs — symbols. It applies rules — its code. It produces outputs — more symbols. At no point does it understand what any of those symbols mean.

The program might pass the Turing Test. It might produce responses that fool every judge who ever tries it. But Searle's argument is: so what? The person in the room fooled the person outside, and the person in the room understood nothing.

Searle called this the distinction between *syntax* and *semantics*. Syntax is the shape of symbols — how they're arranged. Semantics is their meaning. Computers, he argued, can only ever manipulate syntax. Semantics — actual meaning — is something else entirely.
