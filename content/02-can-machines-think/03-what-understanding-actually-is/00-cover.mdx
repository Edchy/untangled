By now you've met Turing's test and the programs that tried to pass it. You've seen machines prove theorems and solve puzzles. Something nags: are they doing what we do when we understand something, or are they doing a very convincing impression of it?

In 1980, a philosopher named John Searle built a thought experiment designed to drive that question into a corner. It's called the Chinese Room. It doesn't resolve the question — nothing does — but it makes the question precise in a way that changes how you think about it.

This chapter is about understanding: what it would mean for a machine to have it, what it would mean for us to know, and why the answer matters more now than it ever has.
