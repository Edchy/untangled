Before the summer was over, Newell and Simon arrived with something remarkable: a working program.

They called it the Logic Theorist. It had one job: prove mathematical theorems.

Specifically, it worked through the first 52 theorems in *Principia Mathematica* — the landmark 1910 work in which Bertrand Russell and Alfred North Whitehead painstakingly laid the logical foundations of mathematics.

The Logic Theorist proved 38 of those 52 theorems. For one of them, it found a proof that was shorter and more elegant than Russell's own.

Newell and Simon submitted that result to a mathematics journal. The editors rejected it, partly because one of the listed authors was a computer program.
