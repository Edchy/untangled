The year is 1956. The place is Dartmouth College, New Hampshire. Ten researchers have gathered for the summer, invited by a young mathematician named John McCarthy.

McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, Herbert Simon, Allen Newell — these are not obscure names. Between them, they would go on to define the field of computer science.

Their proposal contained a sentence that is now famous for its confidence:

> We propose that a 2-month, 10-man study of artificial intelligence be carried out... on the basis of the conjecture that every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it.
> — The Dartmouth Proposal, 1955
