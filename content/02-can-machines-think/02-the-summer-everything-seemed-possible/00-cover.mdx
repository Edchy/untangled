Six years after Turing's paper, ten men gathered at a college in New Hampshire for the summer. They called their project the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence. They had a proposal, a budget, and a conviction: that every aspect of intelligence could, in principle, be described precisely enough for a machine to simulate it.

They thought they'd be done by September.

This chapter is about what they built — which was genuinely impressive — and about the gap that opened up between what they built and what they thought they were building. It's not a story about failure. It's a story about how hard the problem turned out to be, and why the people who underestimated it were not fools.

- The Dartmouth conference: who was there and what they believed
- The Logic Theorist: a machine that proved mathematical theorems
- What those programs were actually doing: search, symbols, rules
- Why the optimism was understandable — and wrong
