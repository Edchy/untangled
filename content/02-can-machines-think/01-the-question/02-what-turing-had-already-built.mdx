By 1950, Turing had already done things that mattered.

During the Second World War, he led the team at Bletchley Park that cracked the Enigma cipher — the code the German military used to encrypt their messages. Historians estimate the work shortened the war by two years.

Before that, he had described, purely in mathematics, a machine that could compute anything computable. Not a physical machine — a thought experiment. But it laid the theoretical foundation for every computer that has ever existed.

He wasn't a dreamer. He was someone who built things and thought carefully about what they were.
