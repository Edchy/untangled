In 1950, a mathematician sat down and wrote a paper that began with a deceptively simple question: can machines think? It's the kind of question a child might ask. It's also the kind of question that, the more carefully you try to answer it, the more it dissolves into something harder.

The man was Alan Turing. He had already built machines that could do things no human could match for speed or precision. And he was deeply unsatisfied with them. Not because they were slow, but because something was missing. He couldn't say exactly what.

This chapter is about that question. Not the answer. Turing didn't have it, and neither do we. But the question itself is worth examining carefully, because how you ask it shapes everything that comes after.
