In 1950, Turing predicted that by the year 2000, a machine would be able to fool a judge about 30% of the time after five minutes of conversation.

He was roughly right.

But it didn't settle anything. When machines got good enough to pass versions of the test, people didn't say "machines think." They said the test was flawed. They moved the goalposts.

This keeps happening. Every time a machine does something that seemed to require intelligence, we decide that thing wasn't really intelligence after all.

Turing saw this coming. He called it "the argument from disability" — the habit of defining thinking as whatever machines can't currently do.

The question he asked in 1950 is still open. It might always be.
