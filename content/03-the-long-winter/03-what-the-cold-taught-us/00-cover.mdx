Two winters. Decades of effort and two comprehensive collapses. If you squint, it looks like a story of failure. But that reading misses something important.

The winters forced a reckoning. Not just with specific techniques, but with the fundamental assumptions behind them — that intelligence was logical, that knowledge could be fully encoded, that the right rules, given enough of them, would produce understanding. Each of those assumptions turned out to be wrong in interesting ways.

Something else was happening during the cold years. Quieter work, less funded, less celebrated. Researchers who had given up on logic and rules and started asking a different question: what if machines didn't follow rules at all? What if they learned?

The winters didn't end AI. They ended a particular idea of what AI was supposed to be. What came next grew from the wreckage.
