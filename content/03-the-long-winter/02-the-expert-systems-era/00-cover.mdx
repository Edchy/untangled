After the first winter thawed, a new approach emerged — one that seemed, for a while, to actually work. Instead of teaching machines to reason from scratch, researchers would give them the rules. Thousands of them. Carefully encoded expertise, extracted from human specialists and written into logic.

Expert systems were real products, used in real hospitals and factories. They diagnosed diseases. They configured computers. They approved loans. For a decade, they were the dominant form of commercial AI, and the optimism returned.

But expert systems had a hidden flaw, one that only became visible at scale. They knew what they knew — and nothing else. Ask them something slightly outside their rules and they failed, confidently. Maintaining them was like painting a bridge: by the time you finished, the start needed repainting.

The second winter was quieter than the first. No single report, no dramatic collapse — just a slow recognition that the approach had limits, and that those limits were fundamental.
