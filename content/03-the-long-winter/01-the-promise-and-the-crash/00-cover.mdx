The 1950s were a moment of extraordinary confidence. The people building AI weren't guessing — they were announcing timelines. Ten years. Twenty at most. The machines would think, reason, converse. It felt like an engineering problem, not a philosophical one.

The early results seemed to justify the optimism. Programs that proved mathematical theorems. A machine that learned to play checkers better than its creator. Systems that could hold a limited conversation. Each success felt like a foothold on a very tall cliff.

But the cliff kept getting taller. What worked beautifully on small, tidy problems collapsed on real ones. Language required the entire world as context. Reasoning required knowledge, and knowledge turned out to be effectively infinite.

The funding was always going to run out eventually. This chapter is about the gap between what was promised and what was possible — and why that gap was so hard to see from the inside.
