Rules are more powerful than they look.
The English language has twenty-six letters. Every novel, every poem, every scientific paper ever written in English is a different arrangement of those same twenty-six shapes. The rules for combining them are simple. What you can say with them is not.

This is how complexity tends to work. Not through infinite variety at the bottom, but through a small number of simple rules, applied again and again in different combinations. The richness comes from the arrangement, not from the pieces.

A computer reasons the same way.
There are exactly three logical operations that underlie all of computing. AND. OR. NOT. That is the complete set. Every program that has ever run on any machine, every calculation, every decision, is some combination of those three rules applied at enormous scale.

This chapter works through that from the ground up. You'll see how connecting two switches together lets them answer a question. How those questions chain into something that can add numbers. How addition leads to memory. And how memory is the thing that turns a switch into a machine that can, in its own way, think.
By the end, you'll have seen, from scratch, how thinking machines think.

- AND, OR, NOT: the only three gates there are
- Chaining: how gates answer bigger questions
- Comparison: building logic from scratch
- Addition: arithmetic in binary
- Memory: how a circuit holds its state
